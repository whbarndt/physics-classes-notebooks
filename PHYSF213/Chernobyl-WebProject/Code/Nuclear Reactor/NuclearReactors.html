The Balance

The primary problems with maintaining balance inside a nuclear reactor comes down to two things: After fission occurs, the energy of the neutron is very large and, because of quantum mechanical reasons, the chances of it splitting the other nuclei is much less. If you are able to slow it down to increase the chance it will split another nuclei, the problem is now slowing down the chain reaction itself so that the heat produced by both the fission and the radioactive decay of the newly formed isotopes won't exponentially increase, which can eventually melt pretty much anything if it gets hot enough. This is what we call a meltdown.

Therefore the way to find balance in a nuclear reactor is to control the neutrons. There are two ways of doing this:
1. To absorb neutrons: decreasing the radioactivity/heat
2. To moderate neutrons: increasing the radioactivity/heat (seems a bit counter-intuitive huh?)

Absorbing neutrons is most commonly done using the element of Boron, usually in the form of a control rod. In the reactor above you can see the control rods inside the reaction chamber. Heavy water (Deuterium/Tritium) can also be used as an absorber, however it is very expensive to make.

The reason why we call 'increasing radioactivity' moderating is due to those quantum mechanical reasons stated before. We need to moderate the neutrons in order for fission to be achieved. The most common moderator is actually light water however graphite is also occasionally used. It is important to note however that light water also has a chance of absorbing neutrons.

This is the delicate balance of a nuclear reactor and these are what the control rods in a reactor are mean't to regulate.

Quantitative Analysis of Energy Produced by Fission:

There are many types of nuclear reactors, one of which you can see on the left. They can differ in the fuel used, the way they cool the reactor, the materials used both in construction but also moderation, and shape but generally use the same underlying principles.

In general nuclear reactors are very simple in how they work. Heat (thermal energy) is generated by nuclear reactions inside the reactor that turns water into steam which then goes through a turbine causing it to spin (mechanical energy). This spin of the turbine is then transferred into electrical energy for use.

Fundamentally, that's how nuclear fission reactors work. Even if it sounds simple in principle, these reaction processes need to be carefully balanced in order for this to work.
