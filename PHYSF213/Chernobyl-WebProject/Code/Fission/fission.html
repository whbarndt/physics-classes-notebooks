Nuclear Fission and Energy Production


Nuclear Fission is a nuclear reaction/process that results with the nucleus of an atom being split up into smaller parts that can consist of lighter nuclei, free neutrons, and radiation. The mass of the original is always greater than the sum of its parts. So where did that mass go? Energy!

This energy is represented as the binding energy of the nucleus. Which stays consistent with conservation of energy. This is the reason why we can generate energy using nuclear reactors. However, only certain nuclei can sustain a chain reaction that will consistently produce this energy. This is because you need a nuclei that, once split, will cause more even nuclei to split from the neutrons that are produced.

The nuclei that is most commonly used in these type of nuclear fission chain reactions is the isotope Uranium -235.




































When Uranium undergoes fission via an accelerated neutron, it will usually split into two smaller nuclei (the most probable being 95 Kryton and 137 Barium, despite the above clip) along with a couple neutrons (the average being about 2.43) and a release of energy that come in the form of heat and gamma rays.
